Date: 2026-07-12; latency remeasured 2026-07-13
Accuracy source commit: `7e01d63e4c9d9d72a2f95638c315616c7901f716`
Compiler supplement source: `a42b31a1013b`
Latency session source: `dd61240c661d`

## Questions

1. Does checkpoint export preserve the PyTorch detector through ONNX Runtime and TensorRT?
2. How do query reduction and Cascade pruning change localization, recall, object-size, and class
   metrics after strongly typed FP16 conversion?
3. Which measured configurations provide useful throughput/quality operating points?

## Protocol

- COCO: D-FINE-M on all 5,000 `val2017` images.
- Cross-domain panel ("RF4"): D-FINE-S fine-tuned independently on four RF100-derived datasets,
  75 epochs, seed 42, FP32 training, best checkpoint selected by validation AP.
- Test labels were excluded from training and checkpoint selection.
- Input: RGB, 640×640 stretch, `/255`.
- Detection score threshold: 0.001. Decode retains up to 300 candidates.
- Decision metrics: standard COCO bbox AP and AR@1/10/100.
- FP32 comparison: TF32 disabled in PyTorch, ONNX Runtime, and TensorRT.
- Production comparison: strongly typed `slim` ONNX and TensorRT engine.
- The latency supplement reused the campaign's exact ONNX and engine artifacts.

The four custom datasets are a cross-domain stress panel, not an RF100 benchmark. Each source is
CC BY 4.0 and retains its published train/validation/test split.

## Throughput surface

D-FINE-M, RTX 4070 Ti SUPER, TensorRT 10.13, 640×640, dynamic profile 1/1/8 (min/opt/max batch),
native C++ image-to-detections with CPU decode. Each cell is the median of five independent 500-iteration
process rounds interleaved across configurations, with 50 warm-up iterations per round; the range
column is the min–max across rounds, and Δ percentages are computed from the unrounded medians. A gate confirmed GPU utilization ≤3% and no compute
processes before every step. Environment: WSL2 Linux 6.18, SM 8.9, driver 581.15, CUDA runtime
12.8.90, and TensorRT 10.13.3.9.post1. These rounds supersede the first latency session, whose
GPU was concurrently loaded by a live video stream on the host; that session's reports remain
preserved but are not decision evidence.

| Graph | b8 img/s | Δb8 | b8 range |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| `base` | 533 | — | 492–537 |
| `Q200` | 560 | +5.1% | 558–568 |
| `C300→150` | 564 | +5.9% | 523–569 |
| `C300→100` | 576 | +8.1% | 555–579 |
| `Q200→C100` | 585 | +9.7% | 571–587 |

## Runtime modes and serving profile

The preset table uses the production 1/1/8 profile and the default enqueue path. The same session
measured the two deployment axes the runtime exposes — a batch-8-optimized engine profile (1/8/8)
and full-pipeline CUDA Graph capture (FP32-output engines built with `--cuda-graph`; the `-g0`
name suffix in [artifact identity](../NAMING.md)) — plus `max`, the historical
record configuration (`Q200→C100` with `--eval-idx 2`).

| Config | Profile | Mode | b1 img/s | b1 p50 ms | b8 img/s | Δb8 vs `base` |
|---|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| `base` | 1/1/8 | CUDA graph | 432 | 2.31 | 558 | +4.7% |
| `base` | 1/8/8 | enqueue | 250 | 3.99 | 564 | +5.9% |
| `Q200→C100` | 1/1/8 | CUDA graph | 475 | 2.11 | 639 | +19.9% |
| `Q200→C100` | 1/8/8 | enqueue | 263 | 3.80 | 637 | +19.6% |
| `max` | 1/1/8 | CUDA graph | 501 | 1.99 | 654 | +22.7% |
| `max` | 1/8/8 | enqueue | 270 | 3.71 | 662 | +24.2% |

Against the same graph's 1/1/8 enqueue path in the preset table, CUDA Graph capture removes 34%
(`base`, 3.49→2.31 ms) to 38% (`Q200→C100`, 3.41→2.11 ms) of batch-1 latency; the 1/8/8 profile
buys batch-8 throughput at a batch-1 cost. The fastest measured points of the study are `max`
under graph capture at batch 1 (1.99 ms per image) and `max` on the 1/8/8 profile at batch 8
(662 img/s).

`max` accuracy on full COCO `val2017`, measured with the same chain as the presets: PyTorch
54.194, ORT raw 54.195, TRT FP32 54.195, TRT `slim` 54.069 AP (PyTorch→`slim` −0.125). Its
`slim` delta against `base` is −0.964 points — roughly twice the `Q200→C100` movement — because `--eval-idx 2` drops a
decoder layer rather than pruning queries. Its RF4 cross-domain deltas are unmeasured.

## Cascade diagnostic

The frozen PyTorch FP32 diagnostic asks which final true-positive queries survive a hypothetical
prune after decoder layer 1. It explains the mechanism; production ranking uses the exported
TensorRT `slim` AP above. The table below is computed on the 300-query graph (the `C300→*`
initial condition), with object sizes taken in original-image pixels.

| Keep | Removed final TPs, IoU .75 | Removed small-object TPs |
|---:|---:|---:|
| 150 | 2.537% | 3.643% |
| 100 | 4.750% | 7.233% |
| 75 | 6.482% | 10.217% |

At K=100, 1,071 of 1,376 removed final TPs were already box-and-class valid at the prune layer;
305 were not jointly valid. On the 200-query graph — the `Q200→C100` initial condition — the
K=100 removal rate is 3.498% overall and 5.534% for small objects. The diagnostic is
counterfactual and FP32-only, so it is not used as a precision or speed proxy.

## PyTorch compiler baseline

On the same COCO model and GPU, compiled FP32 preserved AP within −0.0008 points and improved
measured steady-state E2E throughput by 1.67× at batch 1 and 1.17× at batch 8. The environment was
WSL2 Linux 6.18, driver 581.15, PyTorch 2.9.1+cu128, CUDA 12.8, and cuDNN 9.10.2; the GPU was
otherwise idle. Compiled first calls took 46–63 seconds and are reported separately from steady
state. The four eager/compiled FP32/FP16 rows are in
[Benchmarks](../BENCHMARKS.md#pytorch-baselines).

## Observed operating points

- `base` retains all 300 queries and is the unpruned production point.
- `Q200` provides the smallest measured quality movement with a +5.1% batch-8 gain.
- `C300→150` and `C300→100` give monotonic batch-8 gains (+5.9%, +8.1%); `C300→100` carries the
  largest recall and small-object movement of the cascade-only presets.
- `Q200→C100` is the fastest preset: +9.7% batch 8, with the largest quality movement.
- The 1/8/8 serving profile and CUDA Graph capture stack on top of any preset: up to 662 img/s at
  batch 8 (`max`, 1/8/8) and 1.99 ms at batch 1 (`max`, graph mode).

Users should apply the same report chain to their own checkpoint and test set, then select the
fastest graph that satisfies their AP, recall, object-size, and class budgets.
